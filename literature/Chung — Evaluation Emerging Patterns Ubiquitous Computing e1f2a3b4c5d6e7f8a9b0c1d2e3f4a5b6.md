# One Sentence
Develops 45 ubicomp "pre-patterns" (spanning genres, spaces, interaction techniques, and privacy), then evaluates their usefulness with 16 pairs of designers in a controlled study.

---

# More Sentences
The authors extend Landay & Borriello (2003) by formalizing 45 pre-patterns across four categories: application genres, physical-virtual spaces, interaction techniques, and privacy mechanisms. They evaluate the patterns with 16 pairs of designers tasked with designing a location-enhanced application, comparing pattern-aided vs. unaided conditions. Results show that patterns help designers cover a broader design space and communicate more precisely, but the quality of final designs was not significantly different. This is one of the very few controlled empirical evaluations of design pattern utility in HCI.

---

# Key Points

### 45 pre-patterns, 4 categories
Genres (e.g., reminder, annotation), spaces, interaction techniques, privacy — a structured taxonomy at screen/system level.

### Controlled study: 16 pairs of designers
Pattern-aided designers explored more of the design space; final solution quality did not differ significantly from control.

### "Pre-pattern" framing
Explicitly marked as "pre-patterns" — not yet validated to the stability of Alexander's patterns, but documented and testable.

# Other Notes
The only controlled evaluation study in the full candidate set. Sets the methodological standard PatternGenUI's evaluation should aim to match or exceed. The null result on design quality is important: patterns may improve *process* (coverage, communication) without necessarily improving *output* quality.

# Take-Away
- **Most important precedent for PatternGenUI's evaluation design**: pattern-aided vs. unaided comparison, measuring both process and output quality.
- The process/output distinction should inform how PatternGenUI frames its ablative study hypotheses.
