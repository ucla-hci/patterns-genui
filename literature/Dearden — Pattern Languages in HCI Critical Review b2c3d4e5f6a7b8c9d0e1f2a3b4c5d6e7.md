# One Sentence
Surveys ~40 HCI pattern projects and finds almost no empirical validation of their design utility, calling for formal evaluation methods.

---

# More Sentences
Dearden and Finlay systematically examine pattern catalogs published up to 2005, analyzing their structure, scope, derivation, and evidence base. Their central finding is that pattern languages in HCI are almost never empirically evaluated — most rely on expert authority. They also map the diversity of pattern template structures, noting no consensus on what fields a pattern must contain. The paper is the field's most-cited methodological critique.

---

# Key Points

### Almost no empirical validation
> "Relatively few evaluations of how useful pattern languages are in user interface design" exist, and those that do are small-scale lab studies lacking ecological validity.

### Diversity of template structures
Pattern templates vary widely: some include "forces," "rationale," or "related patterns"; others only name/problem/solution. No standard has emerged.

### Derivation is uniformly expert-curated
Across all ~40 projects surveyed, patterns are derived from expert experience, not mined from data or validated with users.

# Other Notes
Currency is a limitation (2006), but the critique stands: as of 2025, empirical validation of pattern utility remains rare. Sets the evaluation bar that PatternGenUI's ablative study must meet.

# Take-Away
- The authoritative survey baseline: use as the anchor "state of the field before empirical approaches" citation.
- The evaluation gap it identifies directly motivates PatternGenUI's ablative study design.
